An Interview With the Founder of Tegger



I finished a semester-long internship with Tegger and will soon be moving to Kasisto, Inc. as an NLP Researcher - but, as I wrap up, I want to share an informal interview I conducted with Renato Carregha, my supervisor and founder of Tegger. As someone who intends to have an organization of my own in the future, I was deeply curious about what goes into setting up and running a tech start-up. Renato was kind enough to let me record the session, and here is the transcript of our short conversation (TL;DR at the end):

(Note: Tegger is a majorly Latin America based ad-tech company that aims to democratize data and give users more control and tangible benefits when sharing their data.)

The Interview



Adi Singhal: Thank you for this, Renato.

Renato Carregha: My pleasure, Adi.

Adi Singhal: Let’s jump right into it! It’s been a real pleasure to work with you this semester. In your opinion, however, what do you think makes Tegger a great place to work at?

Renato Carregha: I would say, inspiring purpose, a dynamic team and youthful innovation.

Adi Singhal: Youthful innovation? We are a very young company.

Renato Carregha: Exactly, and how we leverage very different technologies to try to get to our goal is an asset.

Adi Singhal: So we know how to scramble?

Renato Carregha: Correct. You take different pieces from what works and value emerges. That’s the beauty of it.

Adi Singhal: Okay yeah that makes sense. We're not constrained to traditions and licenses. What works works.

Renato Carregha: Exactly.

Adi Singhal: Do you like the pace at which we are going right now?

Renato Carregha: To be honest, no. I think any founder would tell you that I would like to grow much faster, so I like the pace at which we're doing. I think we're doing some things correctly, but I think in general, in terms of market acquisition, we're still facing issues and we're facing friction that is making us move slower than we should.

Adi Singhal: And what are the most convenient or most desirable ways around this friction?

Renato Carregha: I think it is our products. Essentially product market alignment through fast iterations.

Adi Singhal: I see. The speed at which you align, or course-correct.

Renato Carregha: Yes. I think that actually for early stage startups, probably the only thing that defines that separate success from failure in the vast majority of cases, is that is the speed at which you can iterate - course-correct, like you said. No startup will be perfect from day one, right? Maybe a couple but extremely rarely.

Adi Singhal: Do you think in the course correction, the company is ever being led to a place where - like it's not the spirit you came in with it? As if that's not what you're passionate about and you’re being asked to conform to something that you are not?

Renato Carregha: No, not now. And this is something actually that I learned from my previous startup partner. I think if you were the founder of a company or one of the founders and you don't like something about it - it's entirely your fault, and therefore it's also entirely up to you to change it. If you're unhappy and you work in a large corporation there's only so much you can change before you need to quit and find a new job, but if you're unhappy with your own company - change it.

Adi Singhal: How do you change a company?

Renato Carregha: Excellent question. It's all about finding first the correct team and correct partners. This is something that is really difficult, especially for me. Being able to drop some things that I was already doing or that I thought were correct. In order to change towards the new ones you need to strategize, aim, prioritize constantly and re-prioritize. And then find the correct team and the correct talents to try to get to where you want to be. Usually to get to the speed, where you want to be, you would need either more people who can help you get there, or you need a correct partner in the marketplace - they don't need to necessarily be team members.

Adi Singhal: I see that makes sense. If I wanted to talk about any difficult obstacles that came up in setting up and keeping Tegger running - what would be the top two or three things that will come to mind?

Renato Carregha: Excellent question. I think one was external factors especially at the beginning of Tegger. Well, not the beginning, but the middle we faced a tough, tough situation. A political situation with an established market competitor. They were trying to block us explicitly and this was a big stopper for us because we lost a partner that it took us months to get back. Then maybe the second big obstacle that we faced was a lack of expertise in technology at the beginning of Tegger I was leading the tech team. We had three developers but I can't write a line of code. I didn't understand technically enough to guide them and this was really slowing us down and generated problems that took literally a year or more to solve.

Adi Singhal: At the helm of the ship that is a technical company, not having much technical expertise is an inhibitor? Even though the people around you are good at what they do... you not being able to tell them exactly the right way to do things is a problem?

Renato Carregha: It is yes. I don't think that everyone in the world needs to know how to code. Moreover, I think that you need to have enough knowledge to understand the decisions that you're supporting. Still today we have a strong technical leader to steer them, our CTO. When we need to take an important technical decision he consults me, and I can give very valuable feedback at the soul of the problem but not at the surface. That is a limitation that I have and I think it would have helped Tegger, especially in the early days, if I knew how to do at least some coding. If I had an understanding of architecture and dev-ops and what to look for in an engineer, and things like that.

Adi Singhal: Okay. Speaking about the people you work with - if someone else in a different, alternate universe were to come up with something like Tegger and bring it to where it is now as an individual - would that be possible?

Renato Carregha: I think another individual could definitely do it with the correct team but I believe that every single person is replaceable in a company. If you think that you're not replaceable, it's either hubris or the organization is weak. So, in that sense, I do believe everybody's replaceable. And I think that the team we have now is strong but I still see areas where we could be better. What is valuable about the team that we have right now is that I don't need to worry about technical issues. I do need to worry about technical topics, but how much we're spending on servers and things like that - you know? I don't need to worry about our system running and this took me years to get. Sometimes I used to demo to investors and when I tried to open the APP it would fail. And this is horrible, right? It's humiliating. It really shows that the company is not at a mature state, so I really appreciate right now that I don't have to worry about technical issues. I also appreciate that I have a lot of team members that have initiative and don't need hand holding. I think what is valuable is to have people that are complimentary to you and can challenge you.

Adi Singhal: All right, yeah that makes sense. You mentioned the app failing in front of an investor - Can you also share a quick anecdote about a critical moment of change for Tegger - something that is a story worth telling?

Renato Carregha: When we first conceived Tegger, we got a bit of money right to develop it and they recruited a team and we designed the product and we started building. We had said, or I had said, to the board that the date of March 29 - I'll never forget it this date - I said we're launching March 29. And it took us a huge effort to launch March 29 because development usually is delayed, right? With Mexican developers even more so! *laughs* I was pushing and pushing and pushing and I said, "If we don't launch March 29 all of us will be out of a job, so we better launch March 29. We're going to a warzone. I pushed and pushed and pushed. And I would stay until 2am with them every night for the last week, until we had it and we launched March 29.

Adi Singhal: You did?

Renato Carregha: Of course! We did. We had to. So we launched and we were happy we had some jolts, of course, but we launched. And then literally three days after we launched, the company partner site that we launched our widget on had an enormous PR disaster. A data leak. Our service was that we take your data with your consent and give you perks for it, right? So, even though we had nothing to do with this data leak - this data leak I'm talking about was an international media blast, like Forbes US picked up the story right, it was –

Adi Singhal: A big story...

Renato Carregha: ...was the biggest data leak in Mexican history for a private company. We had to adapt really quickly and we took the dramatic decision of, three days after launching, taking our service offline. Because of what we saw in the press. Especially because someone started associating us with this, which was catastrophic for a young company that had nothing but its reputation on the line. We had nothing to do with it, of course, so we took our product out of the light. And I think this was a moment where it was most apparent how you need to adapt to change.We had launched after huge effort on time. I had pulled my team as hard as I could to accomplish that. And then, once we launched, we had to go offline. Because situations changed after a while, we were able to adapt and we relaunched a couple of months later. After the storm had passed. We have fixed up the bugs by the time and it was definitely… an invigorating experience.

Adi Singhal: That's a good story, that's exactly what I was hoping for…

Renato Carregha: I got tweeted against. A journalist tweeted my name and handle with my picture saying - “see what this guy is doing with your data - he's selling to Cambridge analytica!”

Adi Singhal: Unbelievable. So, I know our biggest client is Cultura Colectiva - an umbrella organization umbrella, like the godfather organization for our company now - it's been the biggest supporter and is still our biggest site. It's been going on for a while - how did that happen? Is it important to have friends like that?

Renato Carregha: In startups - And this is something that took me a while to understand - you need an unfair advantage. It sounds unfair and cruel but startups will not make it without an unfair advantage. If you have great access to some contacts, if you have this amazing IP that you found, if you have different things that give you an unfair advantage in the market - that's what you need. When I came up with the idea for Tegger I knew that it would be an uphill battle if we did not have the support of a big media organization. The idea was dormant. Curiously, Cultura Collectiva looked for me because they wanted a blockchain project they wanted to try. They had no idea what they wanted to do so, I pitched this and they liked it and the founders invested personally. And it gave us access to this big site, where instead of launching on the first day with just a landing page and looking for sites, we launched with a site that had millions of users. This was an unfair advantage. It's one that we exploit.

Adi Singhal: I think that's a very precious piece of advice that I am going to remember for a long time. To start a good company, you need to have an unfair advantage.

Renato Carregha: And the thing is you can find one unfair advantage but you have to make sure to play to your strengths then and align yourself to that purpose.

Adi Singhal: All right, that makes sense! Alright, umm.. one question that I always think about is how to make people work if you can’t afford to pay them. Can you talk about one or two challenges that come with managing people - what’s difficult about managing people at Tegger?

Renato Carregha: It's hard. It’s tough I think in three senses. 1) Believing that you're the boss - really understanding it and letting it sink - it is, at least for me it was, a challenge. If you're too nice, it's not motivating to people. You need to be firm in some points and you need to take decisions and stick to them. Be able to impose some decisions. It's not necessarily with everyone of course - there's people who will be very cooperative etc, but there's people who are resistant and you need to be able to stand your ground and to me that was an issue. 2) Understanding what value you, as a startup, can give to people. I think that's crucial and that took me a while to understand as well. Especially now that all of our people essentially are still unpaid, right? So it's very interesting to be able to get unpaid people because you understand that there may be more value in what you're offering - why would an intelligent person who could get a good paying job work with me? Understanding what the answer to that is important. 3) Sorry this sounds rough but being able to fire people when you need it. You need to know that. If you keep everyone on board just because they have a good attitude or whatever, your ship starts to slow down and it's not motivating for people who are actually good.To see people who are bad face no consequences is discouraging. You need to be able to do it - we've had to fire interns, unpaid interns. People need to see consequences right? That took me a while to understand and it took me a while to really assimilate, but I think it's important as well.

Adi Singhal: So what I'm hearing is you need to have some leverage. As a startup, money cannot do that because you don't have that so things like believing that you're the boss so that respect follows, and rewarding good work and making sure people understand the consequences of bad work…

Renato Carregha: Yeah, consequences are important because if you're making a big effort and you're working for this goal and you're inspired etc - and you see people who are not and they're getting the same treatment as you - it is demotivating. Especially for unpaid people or people who you're going to pay below market, you need to be able to inspire them with your startup story. To be honest that hasn't been an issue at Tegger. I think it's because I can recruit most people - I enjoy what we do and believe in it so I'm able to transmit it to others. So, I guess that's important as well - to be able to inspire people to work with. it's important to give people intangible benefits as well... so to joke a little bit in the meetings and to ask them about their day - all these things we must leverage.

Adi Singhal: Yeah that makes perfect sense to me. No, it's an interesting thing that I want to study more - how do you get people to do things when you're not paying them.

Renato Carregha: yeah we can talk about it another time, You know what I’ve also found - team meetings are really helpful. That's where you can pull people in. Because if you see other people working hard and you see their progress, it gives you a certain sense of accountability. You don't want to be the person who sits at the meeting and has nothing to report in terms of progress.

Adi Singhal: Absolutely. One question I really wanted to ask - what is the greatest single fear of being a founder of a company like Tegger?

Renato - Ah… I think it's letting people down, to be honest. When people invest in a startup right, they know it's risky but they're investing a lot in you. They're investing in the founders and in their capacity and in their vision and in their ethic, etc. If you have a team of people working for you, they are also vouching that you're going to do a good job - so it's very tough if you don't get the results. The thought that you're letting all of these people down - I think that's the scariest thing. I’d rather not be paid, which also happens frequently, you know? Not pay myself than to let all these people down.

Adi Singhal: That’s tough. And this is a good question to end on, actually - what is the greatest single pleasure of being the founder of a company like Tegger?

Renato Carregha: Great question. You know I'm still extremely proud of my team members launching on time. It is one of my greatest professional accomplishments. My product was buggy but it worked and I wanted it on time - nobody thought that we could do it and we did. It was a huge effort. The other thing is - when you tell the story externally and you see people appreciate it and congratulate you - that also feels really good. Because when you're inside, you're swimming for your life - and if you take your head out of the water and you hear, “wow this is interesting” and “wow I like your product, well done” etc, this feels really good.

Adi Singhal: Like when you're trying to swim in one direction and you don't know exactly how you’re performing - taking your head out of the water and hearing people’s feedback about your progress - that must be energizing. It's like oxygen that you need to dive back in.

Renato Carregha: Yeah... it's tough inside but it's also tough outside though, sometimes. In any startup or, at least in my experience, you will get rejected and rejected and rejected. And you apply for grants and accelerators and you get rejected - and that investor says this is not good enough and there's no market fit - and you lose customers - In this case it's important to sometimes feel the acknowledgement that you're doing things well.

Adi Singhal: Yeah, well, you are doing many things well actually and no one can deny that.You're like working with all these NYU graduates here you have tons of applicants that get attracted to your company, your product - that’s fantastic. And, there’s just a charm that you have, you know, you're doing something right - and I think you're very knowledgeable and a very refined person, you know? Very polished and I see nothing but glamour in Tegger’s future. Thank you for giving me your time.

Renato Carregha: Thank you and enjoy. By the way, I'll be in New York in August, so I hope to see there.

Adi Singhal: Let’s meet up and have some coffee!

Renato Carregha: Excellent.

Some Final Thoughts



1) Market alignment is key, so fast iterations for course correction are extremely important.

2) Lack of technical understanding can be a deterrent when starting out as a leader in tech even if you have a very talented team.

3) To manage people effectively, you need to know when to stand your ground and call the shots. Good work should be rewarded and sub-par work needs to have consequences.

4) The small attitudinal effects of high energy and regular team meetings can be important to keep people going when there are no other things to leverage as a young start-up.

5) You need to be comfortable with rejections and losses because they are everywhere - if you’re the kind of person to get bogged down, progress can be extremely difficult.
